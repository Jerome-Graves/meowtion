\section{On-device AI}
\label{sec:on-device-ai}

All classification happens on the collar, in real time, using TensorFlow Lite Micro
(TFLM)~\cite{david2021tflm} with the CMSIS-NN kernels~\cite{lai2018cmsisnn} for the
nRF52840's Cortex-M4F. The models are tiny int8 networks in the spirit of the TinyML
approach~\cite{warden2019tinyml}.

\subsection{Confidence-gated cascade}

Two behaviours can look almost identical to a motion sensor while sounding completely
different: eating and drinking both involve the head down at a bowl. Rather than force a single
model to separate them from motion alone, Meowtion runs a confidence-gated cascade of two
models:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item The \textbf{IMU model} runs continuously on the motion stream. It is cheap and always on.
  \item When the IMU model is \emph{unsure}, a short \textbf{audio model} is invoked to confirm.
        Audio is therefore a deployed signal, used to resolve ambiguity, not merely a training
        aid.
\end{enumerate}

This keeps average power low (the audio path runs only when needed) while recovering the
accuracy that motion alone cannot provide.

\subsection{Matching training and inference}

The single most important correctness property is that the on-device input transform must mirror
the transform used during training, exactly. A model trained on one representation and fed
another at inference will produce confident nonsense. Meowtion enforces this on both paths:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Motion.} The rolling window is normalised per channel: subtract each channel's
        mean over time, then divide by the global maximum absolute value (with a small epsilon),
        and quantise to int8 using the input tensor's own scale and zero-point.
  \item \textbf{Audio.} Inference audio is decimated and encoded to match the training
        representation: 8\,kHz $\mu$-law, produced by the same conversion used to prepare training
        clips. A mismatch here was an early source of confident misclassification.
\end{itemize}

To avoid a large floating-point scratch buffer on a RAM-constrained part, the IMU path quantises
directly from the int16 source in a small number of passes (compute the per-channel mean and the
global max, then quantise), rather than materialising a float window.
\Cref{lst:quant} sketches the approach.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=C,caption={Direct int16-to-int8 quantisation, mirroring training (sketch).},label={lst:quant}]
// pass 1: per-channel mean over the window
// pass 2: global max-abs of (sample - channel_mean)
// pass 3: write int8 = round((x_norm)/scale) + zero_point
//         where x_norm = (sample - channel_mean) / (maxabs + 1e-6)
// scale and zero_point come from the input tensor itself.
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Memory budget}

TFLM uses a fixed tensor arena per model. The arenas are sized to the two networks and are the
dominant RAM cost of inference:

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{Tensor arenas.}
  \label{tab:arenas}
  \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}llX@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Arena} & \textbf{Notes} \\
    \midrule
    IMU (slot 0)   & 16\,KiB & Always-on motion classifier \\
    Audio (slot 1) & 48\,KiB & Invoked only to confirm an uncertain IMU result \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

Enabling inference initially pushed RAM use to roughly 94\,\%; eliminating the float window
scratch buffer (quantising directly from int16) brought it back to the low 80s, leaving headroom
for the BLE stack and the raised main stack.

\subsection{Operator set}

The op resolver is a fixed \texttt{MicroMutableOpResolver}, so only the operators the models
actually use are linked. The Keras \texttt{Conv1D} layers are exported as \texttt{CONV\_2D}
wrapped in \texttt{EXPAND\_DIMS} and \texttt{SQUEEZE}; the missing \texttt{EXPAND\_DIMS} operator was
the cause of an early ``didn't find op'' load failure, and the resolver now includes it along
with the pooling, fully-connected, reshape, softmax, and quantise/dequantise operators the
networks need.
